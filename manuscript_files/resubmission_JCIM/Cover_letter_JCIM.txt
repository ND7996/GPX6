We are pleased to submit our manuscript, "Epistatic Modulation of Sec/Cys Catalysis in GPX6 Revealed by EVB Free-Energy Landscapes," for consideration as an Article in the Journal of Chemical Information and Modeling.

This work addresses how the surrounding sequence and structural background in enzymes modulates the chemical effect of a catalytic-residue substitution. GPX6 provides a natural test case because primate enzymes retain catalytic selenocysteine, whereas several rodent lineages have replaced it with cysteine. Using empirical valence bond free-energy simulations in human and mouse GPX6 backgrounds, we quantify the proton-transfer step that generates the reactive selenolate or thiolate and show that the energetic advantage of selenium is not a fixed local property of the catalytic residue. Instead, the magnitude of the Sec/Cys effect depends strongly on the surrounding protein scaffold.

The main finding is that the same Sec/Cys exchange has different energetic consequences in different homologous scaffolds. Introducing selenocysteine into mouse GPX6 lowers the activation barrier by 2.84 kcal/mol, whereas replacing selenocysteine with cysteine in human GPX6 raises it by 1.43 kcal/mol. Greedy pathway calculations across 20 active-site-proximal substitutions further reveal direction-dependent and epistatic energetic effects. When projected onto the GPX6 phylogeny, these trajectories support a model in which rodent Cys-GPX6 represents a compensatory catalytic regime rather than a simple loss of selenium chemistry.

We believe the manuscript is well suited to JCIM because it combines molecular simulation, empirical valence bond free-energy modeling, sequence analysis, and structural interpretation to produce a computational framework for connecting enzyme catalysis with evolutionary sequence background. The work falls directly within JCIM's scope in computer simulation using molecular dynamics and free-energy methods, molecular modeling of biological systems, and computational analysis of chemical and physical data. More broadly, it provides a systematic protocol for mapping evolutionary catalytic landscapes, with potential relevance for enzyme design strategies based on electrostatic preorganization.
